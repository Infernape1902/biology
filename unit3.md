# Macromolecules 
## Introduction To Macromolecule 
### Introduction: What Are Biological Macromolecules?

Everything living is made up of molecules, and some of the most important molecules in living organisms are called biological macromolecules. The term macromolecule means "large molecule." Interestingly, many of these molecules are found in the foods we eat every day. When you look at a nutrition label and see information about carbohydrates, fats, or proteins, you are actually looking at different types of biological macromolecules.

Your body is built largely from four major groups of biological macromolecules:

- Carbohydrates
- Lipids
- Proteins
- Nucleic Acids

Together, these molecules make up most of the dry weight of a cell. Although water makes up most of a cell's total mass (wet weight), the majority of the cell's solid material consists of these four macromolecule groups.

### The Four Major Biological Macromolecules

Each type of macromolecule performs specific functions that are essential for life.

1. Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates primarily serve as the body's immediate or short-term energy source. They provide fuel for cellular activities and can also contribute to structural functions.

Examples include:

- Maltose (Glucose + Glucose)
- Sucrose (Glucose + Fructose)
- Lactose (Glucose + Galactose)
- Starch
- Cellulose
- Chitin

Some carbohydrates store energy, while others provide structural support in plants and certain organisms.

2. Lipids

Lipids are commonly known as fats, oils, and waxes. They are mainly responsible for long-term energy storage and are important components of cell membranes.

Examples include:

- Fats
- Oils
- Waxes
- Phospholipids
- Steroids

Unlike carbohydrates, lipids store much larger amounts of energy and help insulate and protect the body.

3. Proteins

Proteins are the most functionally diverse macromolecules in living organisms. They perform a wide variety of tasks, including:

- Providing structural support
- Acting as enzymes to speed up chemical reactions
- Sending chemical signals
- Defending the body against pathogens

Examples include:

- Keratin (hair and nails)
- Enzymes
- Hormones
- Antibodies

Many proteins function like tiny molecular machines that carry out specialized jobs within cells.

4. Nucleic Acids

Nucleic acids store and transmit genetic information.

Examples include:

- DNA
- RNA

DNA contains the instructions needed to build proteins and regulate cellular activities, while RNA helps carry out those instructions.

### Monomers and Polymers: The Building Blocks of Life

Most biological macromolecules are assembled from smaller repeating units called monomers.

A monomer is a small molecule that serves as a building block.

A polymer is a large molecule formed when many monomers are joined together.

A simple analogy is:

- Monomer = a bead
- Polymer = a necklace made of many beads

The repeated joining of monomers creates large molecules with complex structures and functions.

Examples of Monomers and Polymers

| Macromolecule | Monomer (Building Block) |
| --------------|--------------------------|
| Carbohydrates | Monosaccharides (simple sugars) | 
| Proteins | Amino acids | 
| Nucleic Acids | Nucleotides |
| Lipids | Fatty acids + glycerol |

For example:

- Many glucose molecules can join together to form starch.
- Many amino acids can join together to form a protein.
- Many nucleotides can join together to form DNA or RNA.

### Why Lipids Are Different

Unlike carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids, lipids generally do not consist of long repeating chains of identical monomers.

For this reason, some scientists do not classify lipids as true polymers or even as true macromolecules. However, because lipids are one of the four major groups of large biological molecules, many textbooks still include them under the macromolecule category.

The important point to remember is:

- Carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids are true polymers.
- Lipids are large biological molecules but usually are not polymers.


### Dehydration Synthesis: Building Large Molecules

Cells must continually build large biological molecules from smaller building blocks. The process used to join monomers together is called dehydration synthesis.

The term can be broken down into:

- Dehydration = removal of water
- Synthesis = building something larger

During dehydration synthesis:

1. Two monomers come together.
2. One monomer loses a hydrogen atom (H).
3. The other loses a hydroxyl group (OH).
4. H and OH combine to form water (H₂O).
5. A new covalent bond forms between the monomers.

As a result, a larger molecule is produced and a water molecule is released.

#### Example: Forming Maltose

Two glucose molecules can join together through dehydration synthesis to form maltose.

The reaction:

Glucose + Glucose → Maltose + Water

As additional glucose molecules continue joining, longer carbohydrate polymers can be formed.

This same general principle is used to build many proteins, carbohydrates, and nucleic acids in living cells.

### Polymer Diversity: Why Structure Matters

Even when polymers are built from similar monomers, they can have very different properties.

For example:

- Starch
- Glycogen
- Cellulose

are all made entirely from glucose molecules.

However, the glucose molecules are connected differently, resulting in dramatically different structures and functions:

- Starch stores energy in plants.
- Glycogen stores energy in animals.
- Cellulose provides structural support in plant cell walls.

Similarly:

- DNA contains four different nucleotide types.
- Proteins are built from twenty common amino acids.

The sequence and arrangement of monomers determine the final structure and function of the polymer.

### Hydrolysis: Breaking Large Molecules Apart

While dehydration synthesis builds molecules, hydrolysis breaks them down.

The word hydrolysis means:

- Hydro = water
- Lysis = breaking apart

Hydrolysis is essentially the reverse of dehydration synthesis.

During hydrolysis:

1. A water molecule is added.
2. A covalent bond within a polymer breaks.
3. One fragment receives a hydrogen atom (H).
4. The other fragment receives a hydroxyl group (OH).

As a result, large polymers are broken into smaller units or individual monomers.

#### Example: Breaking Down Maltose

The sugar maltose can be split back into two glucose molecules through hydrolysis:

Maltose + Water → Glucose + Glucose

This reaction recovers the original glucose monomers that can then be reused by the cell.

### Relationship Between Dehydration Synthesis and Hydrolysis

These two reactions are opposites of each other:

| Dehydration Synthesis | Hydrolysis |
|-----------------------|------------|
| Builds polymers | Breaks polymers |
| Removes water | Adds water |
| Forms covalent bonds | Breaks covalent bonds | 
| Generally requires energy | Generally releases energy |

Living organisms constantly use both processes to build, remodel, and recycle biological molecules.

### The Role of Enzymes

Both dehydration synthesis and hydrolysis reactions are controlled by enzymes.

Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions without being consumed.

Many digestive enzymes specialize in breaking down specific macromolecules.

Examples include:

| Enzyme | Function | 
|--------|----------|
| Maltase | Breaks down maltose |
| Lipase | Breaks down lipids |
| Peptidase | Breaks down proteins |

Digestion begins almost immediately after food enters the mouth. Enzymes in saliva and throughout the digestive system continuously break down large molecules into smaller building blocks that can be absorbed and used by the body.

### Common Misconceptions About Macromolecules
Misconception 1: Macromolecules Are Visible Because They Are "Large"

The term "large" is relative. Biological macromolecules are enormous compared to ordinary molecules, but they are still far too small to be seen with the naked eye.

For example, a large protein such as hemoglobin is still about one million times smaller than a grain of rice.

Misconception 2: All Fats and Carbohydrates Are Unhealthy

Many people mistakenly think that all fats and carbohydrates are bad for health.

In reality:

- Carbohydrates are a major source of cellular energy.
- Lipids are essential for cell membranes, energy storage, and hormone production.

The body cannot function properly without appropriate amounts of both carbohydrates and lipids.

### Concept

Biological macromolecules are the large molecules that form the foundation of life. The four major groups are carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids. Carbohydrates provide short-term energy, lipids store long-term energy and form membranes, proteins perform a vast array of cellular functions, and nucleic acids store genetic information. Most macromolecules are polymers built from smaller units called monomers. Cells construct these large molecules through dehydration synthesis, which joins monomers while releasing water, and break them apart through hydrolysis, which uses water to split chemical bonds. Enzymes catalyze both processes, allowing organisms to build, digest, and recycle biological molecules efficiently. Understanding these fundamental concepts provides the foundation for studying all of biochemistry, molecular biology, and cell biology.



## Carbohydrates 
Carbohydrates are one of the most important biological molecules found in living organisms and are commonly present in everyday foods such as potatoes, rice, fruits, and bread. For example, a medium potato contains a large amount of carbohydrate, mainly in the form of starch, along with smaller amounts of sugars and fiber. These carbohydrates serve different roles: sugars provide quick energy, starch acts as energy storage, and fiber (such as cellulose) provides structural support in plant cells.

Chemically, carbohydrates are made of carbon (C), hydrogen (H), and oxygen (O), typically in a ratio close to 1 carbon : 2 hydrogen : 1 oxygen, similar to water (H₂O). This is why they are called “carbo-hydrates” (carbon + water). They exist in different sizes and structures, but all carbohydrates can be grouped into three main categories: monosaccharides, disaccharides, and polysaccharides.

### Monosaccharides: The Simple Sugars

Monosaccharides are the simplest form of carbohydrates and act as the basic building blocks of more complex carbohydrates. The most important monosaccharide is glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆), which is a major energy source for cells. Other examples include fructose (found in fruits) and galactose (found in milk sugars).

Monosaccharides usually contain 3 to 7 carbon atoms and follow the general formula (CH₂O)ₙ. Their structure includes many hydroxyl (–OH) groups and one carbonyl (C=O) group. Based on the position of this carbonyl group, monosaccharides are classified as:

- Aldoses: carbonyl group is at the end of the chain (e.g., glucose)
- Ketoses: carbonyl group is within the chain (e.g., fructose)

They are also classified by the number of carbons:

- Trioses (3C), pentoses (5C), hexoses (6C), etc.

Even though glucose, galactose, and fructose share the same chemical formula (C₆H₁₂O₆), they are isomers because their atoms are arranged differently. Some are structural isomers (different bonding patterns), while others are stereoisomers (same bonds but different 3D arrangement). These small differences are important because enzymes can distinguish between them very specifically.

### Structure of Glucose and Ring Formation

Although glucose is often drawn as a straight-chain molecule, in reality it mostly exists in a ring form in aqueous solution (over 99% of the time). The ring forms when the oxygen on the hydroxyl group at carbon 5 reacts with the carbonyl carbon (carbon 1). This reaction causes the molecule to “close” into a ring through an internal chemical interaction.

This ring formation is reversible and dynamic, meaning glucose can switch between linear and ring forms in solution.

In its ring form, glucose exists in two variations:

- Alpha (α) glucose: hydroxyl group is positioned downward
- Beta (β) glucose: hydroxyl group is positioned upward

These differences may seem small but are extremely important because they determine how glucose molecules link together to form larger structures like starch or cellulose.

### Disaccharides: Two Sugars Joined Together

Disaccharides are formed when two monosaccharides bond through a dehydration synthesis (condensation) reaction. In this process:

- One sugar loses a hydrogen (H)
- The other loses a hydroxyl group (OH)
- These combine to form water (H₂O)
- A covalent bond called a glycosidic linkage is formed

For example:

Glucose + fructose → sucrose (table sugar) + water

The specific carbons involved in bonding are important and are used to name the linkage. In sucrose, carbon 1 of glucose bonds with carbon 2 of fructose, forming a 1→2 glycosidic bond.

Common disaccharides include:

- Sucrose (glucose + fructose)
- Lactose (glucose + galactose, found in milk)
- Maltose (glucose + glucose)

### Polysaccharides: Long Chains of Sugars

Polysaccharides are large carbohydrate molecules made of many monosaccharides linked together by glycosidic bonds. They can contain thousands of atoms and may be straight or branched chains.

They serve two major biological roles: energy storage and structural support.

#### A. Storage Polysaccharides
##### Starch (Plants)

Plants store excess glucose as starch, which is made of two components:

- Amylose (unbranched chains)
- Amylopectin (branched chains)

Both are made of glucose in the alpha (α) form, linked mainly by 1→4 bonds, with branching in amylopectin created by 1→6 bonds.

Starch is stored in plant parts like seeds and roots and serves as an energy source for both plants and animals when digested.

##### Glycogen (Animals)

In animals, glucose is stored as glycogen, mainly in the liver and muscles. Glycogen is structurally similar to amylopectin but is more highly branched, allowing rapid release of glucose when energy is needed.

When blood sugar levels drop, glycogen is broken down through hydrolysis to release glucose.

#### B. Structural Polysaccharides
##### Cellulose (Plants)

Cellulose is the main structural component of plant cell walls. It is made of glucose molecules in the beta (β) form, linked by 1→4 bonds.

Because of this β configuration:

- Every alternate glucose is flipped
- Chains form straight, rigid fibers
- Multiple chains are held together by hydrogen bonds

This structure gives plants strength and rigidity. Humans cannot digest cellulose because our enzymes cannot break β-linkages, so it functions as dietary fiber.

Some herbivores (like cows and termites) can digest cellulose due to symbiotic microorganisms in their digestive systems.

##### Chitin (Animals and Fungi)

Chitin is a structural polysaccharide found in:

- Insect exoskeletons
- Crustacean shells
- Fungal cell walls

It is similar to cellulose but contains modified glucose units with nitrogen-containing groups, making it strong and protective.

### Glucose: A Central Biological Molecule

Glucose is one of the most important molecules in biology. It is often called “blood sugar” and serves as the primary energy source for cells. It is also the main product of photosynthesis and the main fuel for cellular respiration, where it is broken down to produce ATP (energy currency of the cell).

Glucose has the formula C₆H₁₂O₆ and contains:

- An aldehyde group at carbon 1 (in its linear form)
- Multiple hydroxyl (–OH) groups on other carbons

In solution, glucose naturally forms a ring through an internal reaction involving the hydroxyl group on carbon 5 and the carbonyl carbon on carbon 1. This process involves electron movement that stabilizes the ring structure.

Glucose is also used to build:

- Glycogen (animal storage)
- Starch (plant storage)
- Disaccharides like sucrose

### Dehydration Synthesis: Building Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are built when monosaccharides join together through dehydration synthesis. During this reaction:

A bond forms between sugars
A water molecule is released
A glycosidic bond is created

This process is driven by interactions between atoms, especially oxygen’s attraction for electrons, which helps form stable covalent bonds between sugar molecules.

### Hydrolysis: Breaking Carbohydrates Down

The reverse process is hydrolysis, where water is used to break glycosidic bonds:

- Water is added to the bond
- The bond breaks
- The carbohydrate splits into smaller sugars

This is essential in digestion. For example:

- Starch from potatoes is broken down into glucose
- Sucrose is broken into glucose and fructose

Hydrolysis allows organisms to access stored energy from carbohydrates.

### Fructose vs Glucose: Key Differences

Fructose and glucose share the same chemical formula (C₆H₁₂O₆) but differ in structure, making them isomers.

Key differences include:

- Glucose: aldehyde group at carbon 1
- Fructose: ketone group at carbon 2

In water:

- Glucose forms a 6-membered ring (pyranose)
- Fructose forms a 5-membered ring (furanose)

Despite their similarities, these structural differences affect sweetness, metabolism, and enzyme recognition.

### Concepts

Carbohydrates are essential biological molecules that serve as energy sources and structural materials. They are built from simple sugar units called monosaccharides, which can join together through dehydration synthesis to form disaccharides and polysaccharides. These larger structures can store energy (like starch and glycogen) or provide structural support (like cellulose and chitin). Glucose plays a central role as the main energy molecule in living organisms, and its structure allows it to form different types of carbohydrates. The balance between building (dehydration synthesis) and breaking down (hydrolysis) carbohydrates is fundamental to life, energy metabolism, and digestion.



## Lipids 
Lipids are a broad group of biological molecules that are defined mainly by one key property: they do not mix well with water. Because of this, lipids are described as hydrophobic (water-fearing) or sometimes nonpolar, meaning they lack charged regions that would allow them to interact easily with water. When placed in water, lipids tend to clump together or separate from it.

Despite often being misunderstood as “unhealthy fats,” lipids are essential for life. They perform many critical functions, including energy storage, insulation, cell membrane formation, hormone production, and water resistance in plants and animals. Lipids include several important categories such as fats (triglycerides), waxes, phospholipids, and steroids, each with distinct structures and biological roles.

### Fats and Oils (Triglycerides)

Fats, also known scientifically as triacylglycerols or triglycerides, are one of the most common types of lipids. A triglyceride is made from two main components:

- A glycerol backbone (a small 3-carbon molecule with three hydroxyl groups)
- Three fatty acids (long hydrocarbon chains ending in a carboxyl group)

These components are joined together through a dehydration synthesis reaction, where each fatty acid binds to glycerol, releasing one water molecule per bond. This results in a triglyceride containing three ester linkages, which are the bonds formed between glycerol and fatty acids.

Fatty acids typically vary in length (usually 16–20 carbons in biology) and may differ in structure, giving fats and oils different physical properties.

In the body, triglycerides are stored in specialized fat cells called adipocytes, forming adipose tissue, which serves as the body’s main energy storage system.

### Saturated vs Unsaturated Fatty Acids

Fatty acids differ based on the type of bonds between carbon atoms in their hydrocarbon chains, which strongly affects their shape and physical behavior.

#### Saturated Fatty Acids

Saturated fatty acids contain only single bonds between carbon atoms. Because each carbon is “filled” with the maximum number of hydrogen atoms, they are called saturated. These molecules have straight chains, allowing them to pack tightly together.

As a result, fats rich in saturated fatty acids tend to be solid at room temperature, such as butter.

#### Unsaturated Fatty Acids

Unsaturated fatty acids contain one or more double bonds, meaning they have fewer hydrogen atoms. They are classified as:

- Monounsaturated: one double bond
- Polyunsaturated: multiple double bonds

Most natural unsaturated fats contain cis double bonds, which introduce a bend or “kink” in the chain. This prevents tight packing, making these fats liquid at room temperature, such as vegetable oils like olive oil.

#### Cis vs Trans Fats

Double bonds can exist in two forms:

- Cis configuration: hydrogen atoms are on the same side → causes a bent structure (common in nature)
- Trans configuration: hydrogens are on opposite sides → creates a straighter chain

Trans fats are mostly produced industrially through partial hydrogenation, a process used to make oils more solid. However, trans fats are strongly associated with negative health effects, including heart disease, and are now restricted or banned in many regions.

### Omega Fatty Acids

Omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids are important types of polyunsaturated fats. They are considered essential fatty acids, meaning the human body cannot synthesize them and they must be obtained from food.

They are derived from:

- Alpha-linolenic acid (ALA) → omega-3 source
- Linoleic acid (LA) → omega-6 source

These fatty acids contain multiple cis double bonds, giving them highly bent structures.

Omega fatty acids are important because they act as precursors for signaling molecules that regulate processes such as inflammation, blood clotting, mood, and heart health. Omega-3 fatty acids in particular are linked to benefits such as reduced triglycerides, lower blood pressure, and improved cardiovascular health.

### Biological Roles of Fats

Although fats are often viewed negatively, they are essential for survival. Their key functions include:

- Long-term energy storage (more energy per gram than carbohydrates)
- Thermal insulation to maintain body temperature
- Protection and cushioning of organs
- Absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (such as vitamins A, D, E, and K)

Thus, fats are vital for normal biological function when consumed in appropriate amounts.

### Waxes

Waxes are another type of lipid that are highly water-resistant. They are structurally made of long fatty acid chains linked to alcohols through ester bonds.

Because of their hydrophobic nature, waxes serve as protective coatings in nature. For example:

- Plant leaves use wax to prevent water loss and repel rain
- Bird feathers are coated with wax to prevent soaking

These properties help organisms survive in wet or dry environments by controlling water interaction.

### Phospholipids: The Basis of Cell Membranes

Phospholipids are special lipids that form the structural foundation of cell membranes. Their structure is similar to triglycerides, but with a key difference:

- They contain a glycerol backbone
- They have two fatty acid tails (instead of three)
- The third position is occupied by a phosphate group

This structure makes phospholipids amphipathic, meaning they have:

- A hydrophilic (water-loving) head (phosphate group)
- A hydrophobic (water-fearing) tail (fatty acids)

Because of this dual nature, phospholipids naturally arrange themselves in water to form a bilayer:

- Heads face outward toward water (inside and outside the cell)
- Tails face inward, away from water

This arrangement forms a stable barrier called the cell membrane, which controls what enters and leaves the cell.

Phospholipids can also form micelles, spherical structures that minimize contact between water and hydrophobic tails. This organization increases system stability and is driven by energy and entropy considerations.

### Steroids

Steroids are structurally different from other lipids but are classified as lipids because they are hydrophobic and insoluble in water. All steroids share a characteristic structure of four fused carbon rings.

One of the most important steroids is cholesterol, which plays multiple roles in the body:

- A key component of cell membranes, helping regulate fluidity
- A precursor for steroid hormones, such as:
  - Testosterone
  - Estradiol
  - Cortisol
- A starting material for vitamin D and bile acids, which help digest fats

Cholesterol exists in different forms in the bloodstream, including:

- HDL (high-density lipoprotein): often considered beneficial
- LDL (low-density lipoprotein): associated with higher cardiovascular risk when elevated


### Concepts

Lipids are a diverse group of hydrophobic biological molecules essential for life. They include triglycerides (fats and oils), which store energy; phospholipids, which form cell membranes; waxes, which provide waterproof protection; and steroids, which serve as structural molecules and hormone precursors. The structure of fatty acids—whether saturated, unsaturated, cis, or trans—strongly influences the physical properties and health effects of fats. Phospholipids are especially important because their amphipathic nature allows them to form the membranes that define cells. Overall, lipids play crucial roles in energy storage, cellular structure, signaling, and protection, making them indispensable to biological systems.




## Nucleic Acid 
Nucleic acids are one of the four major biological macromolecules and are essential for the continuity of life. Their primary function is to store, transmit, and express genetic information. The two naturally occurring types of nucleic acids are deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) and ribonucleic acid (RNA). DNA serves as the long-term storage system for hereditary information, passing genetic instructions from parents to offspring. RNA acts as an intermediary and functional molecule that helps convert the information stored in DNA into proteins and regulates many cellular activities.

DNA is often called the "blueprint of life" because it contains the instructions needed to build, maintain, and operate living organisms. RNA, while closely related to DNA, performs a wider variety of functional roles within cells, including protein synthesis and gene regulation.

### Location and Organization of DNA

The location of DNA differs between eukaryotic and prokaryotic organisms.

#### Eukaryotic Cells

In organisms such as plants, animals, and fungi, most DNA is stored inside the nucleus, a membrane-bound structure that protects genetic material. Smaller amounts of DNA are also found in organelles such as:

- Mitochondria (all eukaryotes)
- Chloroplasts (plants and algae)

DNA in eukaryotes is organized into long linear structures called chromosomes.

#### Prokaryotic Cells

In bacteria and other prokaryotes, DNA is not enclosed within a nucleus. Instead, it occupies a specialized region called the nucleoid. Prokaryotic chromosomes are usually:

- Smaller than eukaryotic chromosomes
- Circular rather than linear

Each chromosome contains many genes, which are segments of DNA that carry instructions for producing specific cellular products.

### The Central Dogma of Molecular Biology

One of the most important concepts in biology is the Central Dogma, which describes the flow of genetic information:

DNA → RNA → Protein

Step 1: DNA

DNA stores genetic instructions.

Step 2: Transcription

A gene's DNA sequence is copied into messenger RNA (mRNA).

Step 3: Translation

The mRNA travels to ribosomes, where its nucleotide sequence is translated into a sequence of amino acids, forming a protein.

This process allows genetic information to be expressed as functional molecules that perform cellular tasks.

### Genes Do More Than Encode Proteins

Although many genes encode proteins, not all genes do.

Some genes produce functional RNA molecules such as:

Ribosomal RNA (rRNA)
- Forms part of the ribosome.
- Helps catalyze protein synthesis.

Transfer RNA (tRNA)
- Delivers amino acids to ribosomes.
- Ensures the correct amino acid is added during protein synthesis.

Regulatory RNAs

Examples include:

- MicroRNA (miRNA)
- Small interfering RNA (siRNA)
- Long non-coding RNA (lncRNA)
- Piwi-interacting RNA (piRNA)

These RNAs regulate gene expression and help control cellular functions.

### Nucleotides: The Building Blocks of Nucleic Acids

DNA and RNA are polymers made of repeating monomers called nucleotides.

Each nucleotide consists of three components:

1. Nitrogenous Base

Contains nitrogen and stores genetic information.

2. Five-Carbon Sugar

Acts as the structural center of the nucleotide.

3. Phosphate Group

Provides acidic properties and forms part of the backbone.

When many nucleotides join together, they form a polynucleotide chain.

### Nitrogenous Bases

Nitrogenous bases are divided into two categories based on their ring structures.

Purines (Double-Ring Structure)
- Adenine (A)
- Guanine (G)

Purines contain two fused carbon-nitrogen rings.

Pyrimidines (Single-Ring Structure)
- Cytosine (C)
- Thymine (T) — found only in DNA
- Uracil (U) — found only in RNA

Pyrimidines contain a single carbon-nitrogen ring.

DNA Bases

DNA contains:

- A (Adenine)
- T (Thymine)
- G (Guanine)
- C (Cytosine)

RNA Bases

RNA contains:

- A (Adenine)
- U (Uracil)
- G (Guanine)
- C (Cytosine)

The main difference is that RNA uses uracil instead of thymine.

### Sugars: Ribose vs Deoxyribose

DNA and RNA differ in the sugar component of their nucleotides.

DNA Sugar: Deoxyribose

Contains:

- Hydrogen (H) on the 2' carbon

RNA Sugar: Ribose

Contains:

- Hydroxyl group (-OH) on the 2' carbon

Because RNA contains an extra oxygen atom, it is generally:

- More reactive
- Less stable

DNA lacks this oxygen, making it:

- More chemically stable
- Better suited for long-term information storage

This difference explains the name:

Deoxyribonucleic Acid = Ribose without one oxygen ("de-oxy")

### Phosphate Groups and Why DNA is Acidic

The phosphate group is responsible for the acidic nature of nucleic acids.

In neutral cellular environments:

- Phosphate groups lose hydrogen ions (H⁺)
- Become negatively charged

As a result:

- DNA carries an overall negative charge
- DNA is classified as an acid

The phosphate groups are located on the outside of the DNA molecule, forming part of its structural backbone.

### Formation of Polynucleotide Chains

Nucleotides join together through phosphodiester bonds.

This bond forms between:

- The 3' hydroxyl group of one nucleotide
- The 5' phosphate group of the next nucleotide

This creates a continuous sugar-phosphate backbone.

Directionality

Every nucleic acid strand has direction:

- One end is called the 5' end
- The other is called the 3' end

Sequences are always written from:

5' → 3'

Example:

5'-ATGCGT-3'

New nucleotides are always added to the 3' end during synthesis.

### Structure of DNA

DNA has a unique structure known as the double helix.

It resembles a twisted ladder:

Sides of the Ladder

Made of alternating:

- Sugar
- Phosphate

Together they form the sugar-phosphate backbone.

Rungs of the Ladder

Made of paired nitrogenous bases.

The two DNA strands run in opposite directions, making DNA:

Antiparallel

One strand:

5' → 3'

Other strand:

3' → 5'

This antiparallel arrangement is essential for DNA replication.

### Complementary Base Pairing

DNA strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary bases.

Base-Pairing Rules
| Base | Pairs With |
|------|------------|
| A | T |
| G | C |

Hydrogen Bonds
- A–T forms 2 hydrogen bonds
- G–C forms 3 hydrogen bonds

Because G-C pairs have more hydrogen bonds, they are stronger and more stable.

Example

If one strand is:

5'-AATTGGCC-3'

The complementary strand is:

3'-TTAACCGG-5'

This predictable pairing allows DNA to store and copy information accurately.

### DNA Replication

DNA replication is the process by which DNA copies itself before cell division.

Steps
1. DNA strands separate.
2. Each strand acts as a template.
3. Complementary nucleotides are added.
4. Two identical DNA molecules are produced.

Because of base-pairing rules:

- A always attracts T.
- G always attracts C.

Each original strand contains enough information to rebuild its partner.

This ensures genetic information is accurately passed to daughter cells and future generations.

### RNA: Structure and Characteristics

RNA differs from DNA in several ways.

| Feature | DNA | RNA |
|---------|-----|-----|
| Sugar | Deoxyribose | Ribose |
| Bases | A,T,G,C | A,U,G,C |
| Structure | Double-stranded | Usually single-stranded |
| Stability | More stable | Less stable |
| Main Function | Genetic storage | Gene expression and regulation |

Because RNA is usually single-stranded, it can fold into complex three-dimensional shapes that enable diverse functions.

### Messenger RNA (mRNA)

Messenger RNA serves as the link between DNA and proteins.

Function
1. DNA is transcribed into mRNA.
2. mRNA carries genetic instructions out of the nucleus.
3. Ribosomes read the mRNA sequence.
4. Proteins are synthesized.

Codons

Ribosomes read mRNA three nucleotides at a time.

Each three-base unit is called a codon.

Examples:

- AUG → Methionine (start codon)
- UUU → Phenylalanine

Since there are 4 possible bases:

4 × 4 × 4 = 64 possible codons

These codons specify the 20 amino acids used in proteins.

### Ribosomal RNA (rRNA)

Ribosomal RNA forms the structural and functional core of ribosomes.

Functions include:

- Holding mRNA in place
- Catalyzing peptide bond formation
- Helping assemble proteins

Because rRNA can catalyze reactions, it is considered a ribozyme.

### Transfer RNA (tRNA)

Transfer RNA acts as the translator of the genetic code.

Its functions include:

- Carrying amino acids
- Matching mRNA codons
- Delivering the correct amino acid to the ribosome

tRNA molecules fold into a characteristic three-dimensional shape resembling an "L."

Without tRNA, translation would not be possible.

### Regulatory RNAs

Not all RNA molecules participate directly in protein production.

miRNA and siRNA

These small RNA molecules:

- Bind specific mRNAs
- Prevent translation
- Promote mRNA degradation

This allows cells to finely control gene expression.

Regulatory RNAs play important roles in:

- Development
- Cell differentiation
- Cancer
- Genetic diseases

Scientists are currently exploring their use in medical therapies.

### RNA World Hypothesis

A major evolutionary theory proposes that RNA appeared before DNA.

According to the RNA World Hypothesis:

1. Early life may have relied on self-replicating RNA molecules.
2. RNA could both store information and catalyze reactions.
3. DNA later evolved as a more stable storage molecule.
4. Proteins eventually became the primary catalysts of life.

This hypothesis helps explain why RNA still performs many essential cellular functions today.

### Biological Importance of Nucleic Acids

Nucleic acids are the foundation of heredity and cellular function. DNA stores the genetic instructions that define an organism, while RNA interprets and executes those instructions. Through the processes of replication, transcription, and translation, genetic information is accurately copied, transmitted, and converted into proteins. Proteins then perform most of the work required for life, including catalyzing reactions, building cellular structures, transporting molecules, and regulating biological processes. Without nucleic acids, cells would be unable to inherit information, produce proteins, or sustain life itself.

### Concept 
DNA
- Genetic information storage
- Double-stranded double helix
- Contains A, T, G, C
- Sugar = deoxyribose
- More stable
- Replicates itself

RNA
- Usually single-stranded
- Contains A, U, G, C
- Sugar = ribose
- Less stable but more versatile
- Functions in protein synthesis and gene regulation

Central Dogma

DNA → RNA → Protein

Base Pairing
- A ↔ T (or U in RNA)
- G ↔ C

Major RNA Types
- mRNA = carries genetic message
- rRNA = forms ribosomes
- tRNA = delivers amino acids
- miRNA/siRNA = regulate gene expression

Nucleic acids store, transmit, and regulate genetic information, making them the molecular foundation of heredity and life.
